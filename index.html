Creative Problem-Solving Process  /  Topic A

Preventing the Malicious Use
of Deepfakes on Social Media

A six-step creative strategy using fact-finding, brainstorming, SCAMPER, Six Thinking Hats, evaluation, and implementation planning.

Step 01  /  Fact Finding
What is the actual problem?
Deepfakes use generative AI to synthesise convincing fake video and audio of real people. Their weaponisation on social media is accelerating across three harm vectors:
Political Fabricated statements from world leaders used to manipulate elections or incite conflict.
Personal Non-consensual intimate imagery targeting private individuals — overwhelmingly women.
Financial CEO voice/video fraud and fake endorsements draining consumer trust and funds.
Sources: Sensity AI (2023); EU DSA Impact Assessment; Stanford Internet Observatory. Key assumptions: detection tools lag behind generation tools; platforms currently lack uniform enforcement; public media literacy is low.
High Urgency Cross-border AI-Generated
Step 02  /  Problem Definition
Problem Statement
Social media platforms currently lack a coordinated, technically enforced, and publicly trusted system for detecting and limiting the spread of malicious deepfakes — allowing harmful synthetic media to circulate at scale before harm can be contained.
Why does this matter?

Once viral, content is nearly impossible to fully retract. The damage — to reputation, democracy, and personal safety — persists long after takedowns. A prevention + rapid response approach is required, not detection alone.
Constraints to design around:
Free expression boundaries  /  Cross-jurisdictional law  /  Platform business incentives  /  Public scepticism of censorship
Step 03  /  Idea Finding — Brainstorm
Raw ideas — no filtering yet
Platform Mandatory AI-content labelling at upload; deepfake flag embedded in video metadata.
Detection API Open-access detection API shared across platforms so no single actor gatekeeps.
Consent Layer Biometric consent ledger: public figures can register approved synthetic likenesses.
Media Literacy Mandatory short-form media literacy modules embedded directly into platform onboarding flows.
Legal Fast-track civil injunction for victims without requiring criminal prosecution first.
Provenance C2PA-style cryptographic content credentials: every video carries a verifiable origin chain.
Reporting UX One-tap deepfake report that escalates to a specialist review queue, not generic moderation.
Watermarking Invisible steganographic watermarks embedded by AI generation tools at creation time.

Step 05  /  Solution Finding — Six Thinking Hats
Multi-angle evaluation of the shortlisted ideas
White — Facts
Deepfake detection accuracy reaches ~90% on known models but drops sharply on novel architectures. C2PA provenance already adopted by Adobe, Microsoft, and major camera makers. 96% of deepfake content is non-consensual intimate imagery (Sensity, 2023).
Red — Intuition
A "synthetic passport" on every video feels invasive to creators making legitimate artistic work. Victims want faster action — regulatory timelines feel incompatible with viral speed.
Black — Caution
Detection tools can be evaded. Watermarks can be stripped. Consent frameworks require international legal harmonisation which is slow. Over-enforcement risks suppressing satire and parody.
Yellow — Benefits
Combining provenance + detection + literacy addresses the problem at three distinct layers. The rapid response unit creates accountability without requiring new legislation immediately.
Green — Creativity
What if platforms competed on trust scores instead of engagement rates? A public "synthetic integrity index" could make safety a commercial differentiator rather than a compliance burden.
Blue — Process
No single mechanism is sufficient. The strongest strategy layers technical controls (provenance + detection), platform policy (strike tiers + rapid response), and social infrastructure (literacy + legal support).

Step 06  /  Acceptance Finding — Final Strategy
Recommended
The Three-Layer Deepfake Defence Framework
Layer 1 — Technical
  • Shared cross-platform detection API, open access
  • C2PA content credentials on all video uploads
  • Perceptual hash blocklist to prevent re-upload
  • AI watermarking embedded at generation source
Layer 2 — Platform Policy
  • 3-tier strike system: label, restrict, remove + ban
  • Specialist deepfake review queue (not generic mod)
  • Cross-platform rapid response unit, always active
  • Mandatory incident reporting to centralised registry
Layer 3 — Social + Legal
  • Media literacy embedded in platform onboarding
  • Fast-track civil injunctions for victims
  • Consent-at-source framework for generative tools
  • Public synthetic integrity trust score for platforms
Implementation pathway:   Phase 1 (0-6 months) — Deploy detection API and upload labelling, no new legislation required.   Phase 2 (6-18 months) — Establish rapid response unit via platform coalition; roll out literacy modules.   Phase 3 (18 months+) — Advocate for legal frameworks enabling fast-track victim remedies and consent-at-source mandates.
